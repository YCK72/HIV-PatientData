This dashboard presents correlation analyses from a phone-collected HIV patient dataset. Patients are classified into four groups based on HIV status and presence of chronic pain. All correlations are examined within and across these groups to determine whether relationships are driven by HIV, pain, or their interaction. Statistical tests used: Point-Biserial r (binary vs continuous), Spearman r (ordinal vs continuous), and Chi-Square (binary vs binary). A p-value below 0.05 is considered statistically significant.

Correlation 1: Pain Severity vs Mental Health Conditions

Point-Biserial r · Higher pain rating vs presence of mental health condition

Chronic pain and mood disorders are bidirectionally linked — pain increases psychological distress, and mental health conditions lower pain tolerance and coping ability. This analysis tests whether higher bodily pain ratings correlate with current depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, or other mental health conditions, broken down by group. If the correlation is strong in both pain groups (HIV-Pain and No HIV-Pain) but weak in No HIV-No Pain, it suggests the relationship is driven by pain rather than HIV status.

Correlation 2: Chronic Pain Duration vs Mental Health

Point-Biserial r · Pain lasting 3+ months or 12+ hrs/day vs mental health flags

Pain that persists beyond 3 months is classified as chronic and has a well-documented association with depression and anxiety. This analysis extends Correlation 1 by examining whether the duration of pain — not just its severity — predicts mental health burden. Patients reporting pain for over 3 months or 12+ hours daily are compared against mental health condition flags across all four groups.

Correlation 3: ART Adherence vs Pain

Spearman r · Missed ART doses vs pain severity and duration (HIV patients only)

Poor ART adherence in HIV patients can result in suboptimal viral suppression, which drives systemic inflammation and neuropathic pain. Conversely, patients experiencing high pain levels may struggle to maintain consistent medication routines. This bidirectional relationship is tested here using Spearman correlation between missed doses and pain rating, and point-biserial correlation between missed doses and chronic pain flags. Only HIV-positive patients are included.

Correlation 4: ART Drug Class vs Peripheral Neuropathy

Chi-Square · Drug class presence vs peripheral neuropathy diagnosis

Certain ART drug classes — particularly older NRTIs (Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors) — are known to cause peripheral neuropathy as a side effect through mitochondrial toxicity. This analysis uses chi-square tests to check whether patients on each drug class (NRTI, INSTI, NNRTI, PI) show significantly higher rates of peripheral neuropathy, providing a real-world pharmacovigilance signal from this dataset.

Correlation 5: Metabolic Comorbidity Clustering

Chi-Square & Point-Biserial r · Weight, diabetes, HBP, heart disease, kidney disease

Metabolic syndrome — the co-occurrence of obesity, hypertension, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease — is a well-established clinical cluster. HIV itself and certain ART regimens (particularly PIs) can accelerate metabolic dysfunction. This analysis tests whether these conditions co-occur at higher rates in HIV groups compared to non-HIV groups, and whether the clustering pattern differs by pain status.

Correlation 6: Age vs Total Comorbidity Burden

Spearman r · Age vs count of current conditions

Older age naturally accumulates more chronic conditions. However, HIV-positive patients often show accelerated aging — accumulating comorbidities at younger ages than HIV-negative peers due to chronic immune activation and long-term ART exposure. If HIV groups show a steeper age-comorbidity slope than non-HIV groups, that supports the accelerated aging hypothesis in this sample.

Correlation 7: Age vs Pain Severity

Spearman r · Age vs bodily pain rating across groups

Pain prevalence and severity generally increase with age due to degenerative joint changes, reduced tissue resilience, and accumulating conditions like arthritis. In HIV patients, neuropathic pain from long-term infection and ART exposure may amplify this age-pain relationship. This scatter plot with trend lines shows whether older patients report higher pain, and whether this trend differs between HIV and non-HIV groups.

Correlation 8: Smoking vs Pain Severity

Point-Biserial r · Cigarette & e-cigarette use vs pain rating

Smoking is associated with heightened pain sensitivity through nicotine-driven neurological changes and increased systemic inflammation. Smokers report higher pain intensity and are more likely to develop chronic pain conditions. This analysis tests whether current cigarette or e-cigarette use correlates with higher pain ratings, broken down by group to see if this effect is amplified in HIV-positive patients.

Correlation 9: Sleep Condition vs Pain & Mental Health

Point-Biserial r · Sleep disorder vs pain severity and mental health flags

Sleep disruption and chronic pain form a well-documented bidirectional cycle — pain interrupts sleep, and poor sleep lowers pain thresholds and increases emotional reactivity, worsening mood disorders. This analysis tests whether patients with a current sleep condition report higher pain severity and greater rates of depression and anxiety, and whether this three-way relationship (pain, sleep, mood) is stronger in specific groups.

Correlation 10: Polypharmacy vs Pain & Hospitalization

Spearman r & Box Plot · Number of medication classes vs outcomes

Polypharmacy — being on multiple medication classes simultaneously — is both a marker of disease burden and an independent risk factor for adverse outcomes including drug interactions, side effects, and hospitalizations. Patients on more medication classes likely have more complex health profiles. This analysis tests whether medication count correlates with higher pain scores and whether hospitalized patients are on significantly more medication classes.

Correlation 11: Lower Limb Pain Region vs Peripheral Neuropathy

Chi-Square · Feet/ankle and thigh/knee/leg pain vs neuropathy diagnosis

Peripheral neuropathy typically manifests as tingling, burning, or pain in the extremities — particularly the feet and lower legs (a "stocking-glove" distribution). If patients reporting foot/ankle or leg pain are significantly more likely to also have a peripheral neuropathy diagnosis, this serves as an internal consistency check for the dataset and a clinically meaningful signal. This relationship should be especially pronounced in HIV-Pain patients where neuropathy is more prevalent.

Correlation 12: Comorbidity Prevalence Across All Four Groups

Grouped bar chart · Prevalence (%) of 10 key conditions by group

This overview chart shows the percentage of patients in each group currently living with 10 key conditions: hypertension, heart disease, diabetes, cancer, kidney disease, sleep disorders, depression, anxiety, peripheral neuropathy, and stroke. Comparing prevalence across all four groups simultaneously reveals which conditions are driven by HIV status, which by pain, and which by both — providing a high-level map of the disease burden distribution in this sample.
